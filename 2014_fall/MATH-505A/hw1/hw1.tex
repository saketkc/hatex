\problemAnswer{ % Answer
At the start of the tournament we have $2^n$ players to begin with. At
each round there will be \textbf{one} winner emerging from each of the pairs while the other gets 'knocked out'. One possible configuration for the first round of the tournament would be: $Player_1$ v/s $Player_2$; $Player_3$ v/s $Player_4$;...;, $Player_{(2^n-1)}$ v/s $Player_{(2^n)}$. At the end of first round, there are exactly $\frac{2^n}{2}=2^{n-1}$ winners and an equal number of knocked out players. 

At round 1 the set of $2^n-1$ pairs can be represented as :$P_1, P_2, P_3, P_4, .., P_{2^{n}-1}$. The total number of such pairs is $2^n$ divided by $2$ since each pair has 2 players. The outcome of first round can generate two values for each of these pairs depending on who amongst the two players is the winner. For e.g. $Player_1$ can win while playing in $P_1$ or $Player_2$ can, Thus total number of such configurations for the round 1 would be $2 * 2 * 2 *...* (2^n-1)$ times 
which is equal to $2^{2^{n-}}$. 
Now at round 2 we would have $2^{n-2}$ pairs of players to play with and the possible configuration for choosing a winner of such a configuration is $2^{2^{n-2}}$

Thus, the sample space representing how the winners are chosen (or the knocked out persons are knocked out) can be calculated by multiplying configurations as obtained in $round_1$ $round_2$, ... $round_n$ by the \textbf{rule of product} as:
$2^{2^{n-1}} * 2^{2^{n-2}} * .... * 2^{1}$ = $X$

\begin{center}
\begin{equation}
log_2 X = 2^{n-1} + 2^{n-2} + ... + 1
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
log_2 X = \frac{2^{n-1+1}-1}{2-1}
\end{equation}

\end{center}

\textbf{Thus $X$ = $2^{2^n-1}$}

		\end{equation}

		\label{5a2}
		 (A\cup B) \cap (A\cup C)	\subseteq	A \cup (B \cap C) 
		\end{equation}

			\end{multline}
			
			Hence combining \ref{152c5} and \ref{152c6} and doing similar such operations we get: \\
			
				\begin{equation}
					\label{152c7}
					P(A_1 \cap A_2 \cap A_3.. \cap A_n) = P(A_1)P(A_2 | A_1)P(A_3 | A_1 \cap A_2).....P(A_n| A_1 \cap A_2 \cap A_3 .. \cap A_{n-1})
				\end{equation}
			as required.\\
			
			Hence proved.
			}
	\end{homeworkProblem}
